01

Before Your Keys

The earlier you start planning, the fewer surprises you'll have. Most homeowners who struggle with renovation started too late — not because they didn't have time, but because they didn't know what to decide and when.

Understanding your home & lifestyle

Before talking to any ID, spend time answering these for yourself. Your answers directly shape your layout, storage, and budget priorities.

Who is living here?
  • How many people — and their ages
  • Couple only, or with children / parents?
  • Is there a plan to have children in the next 3–5 years?
  • Will elderly parents ever move in?
  • Any pets, or plan to get one?
How do you live day-to-day?
  • Do you work from home? How often?
  • Do you cook frequently, or mainly for weekend meals?
  • Do you entertain guests at home?
  • Any hobbies that need dedicated space? (Music, art, fitness)
  • Night owl or early riser? (Affects lighting and soundproofing)
Room-by-room planning
RoomDecide this earlyWhy it matters
Master bedroom Bed size, wardrobe side, TV or no TV, study nook? Bed size determines wardrobe width. TV point and fan point must be planned before electrical works.
Bedroom 2 / 3 Purpose: guest room, study, nursery, or shared kids room? Different purposes need different electrical points, storage types, and floor clearances.
Living room TV size and wall, sofa layout, dining size, display storage? TV size sets the sofa distance. Dining size sets the space left for living. Decide before carpentry is drawn.
Kitchen Cooking style, appliances needed, open or closed kitchen? Heavy cooking needs better ventilation. Appliance list affects countertop cutouts and plumbing points.
Bathrooms Rain shower, handheld, or both? Bathtub? Instant or storage heater? Shower type affects plumbing layout. Bathtub needs floor-load check and extra waterproofing scope.
Yard / utility Washer only, or washer + dryer? Yard washing area? Dryer type (vented vs condenser) affects wall penetration. Points must be planned before tiling.
Needs vs wants — be honest about both
NEEDS — must have
  • ✓Enough storage for your actual belongings — measure and count before planning
  • ✓The right number of beds for who is sleeping here
  • ✓Functional kitchen for how you actually cook
  • ✓Sufficient bathroom count for the household routine
  • ✓Adequate electrical points where you actually use devices
WANTS — be selective
  • ·Feature walls and display cabinets — beautiful but consume budget fast
  • ·Island / breakfast bar — measure if you have the space first
  • ·Full false ceiling throughout — not all rooms benefit equally
  • ·Large-format tiles — higher cost and more waste than standard sizes
  • ·Rain shower — check if your water pressure supports it

Note: Electrical works and light fittings are typically not included in standard renovation quotes — always ask your ID to confirm what is and isn't covered before signing.

Shortlisting your ID or contractor
DO
  • ✓Request itemised quotes, not lump sums — compare scope line by line
  • ✓Check 3–5 IDs before deciding
  • ✓Ask who handles permit submissions and their typical timeline
  • ✓Visit a completed or ongoing project in person
  • ✓Get everything in writing — verbal agreements are not binding
DON'T
  • ✕Sign because the quote is cheapest — scope may be less
  • ✕Pay more than 10% deposit before work begins
  • ✕Rely on what was said verbally — it will not hold up if disputed
  • ✕Rush the shortlisting just because keys are coming soon

03

Renovation Phases

Renovation runs in a fixed sequence. Each phase depends on the previous one being done. Decisions made after a phase starts always cost more to change.

  • 01
    Hacking & Demolition
    Existing floor and wall finishes, built-ins, and structures to be removed are hacked out. This is when structural surprises in resale units surface — your ID should do a site visit during or right after this phase.
    Good time to visit: confirm what you see matches what was quoted
  • 02
    Masonry
    New brick or block walls are built, wet areas are waterproofed (mandatory in all hacked wet zones), floors are screeded to level, and tiles are laid. All wet area positions must be confirmed before this phase — plumbing points are set here.
    Confirm all wet area layouts before masonry begins — changes here affect plumbing
  • 03
    Plumbing
    Water supply and waste pipes are run to all wet areas. There are two approaches: concealed works (pipes hidden within walls and floors — cleaner finish, more work involved) or exposed works (pipes run on surface — more accessible for future servicing). Your ID will advise based on your flat's existing condition and design direction.
    Pre-2002 flats: original exposed pipes are common — discuss with your ID whether to conceal or retain
  • 04
    Electrical Works
    Wiring, conduits, and the DB box are upgraded or amended. Like plumbing, this can be done as concealed works (wiring run inside walls and ceiling — requires hacking and plastering) or exposed works (surface trunking — faster, more accessible). Confirm every socket, switch, and light point position before walls are plastered over.
    Lock in all socket and switch positions now — relocating them after plastering is additional cost
  • 05
    False Ceiling & Dry Works
    Plasterboard ceilings, partition walls, and any dry-wall structures are constructed. Confirm all light positions, aircon trunking routes, and speaker or cabling runs before the ceiling closes up. Note that false ceiling reduces room height — check your existing ceiling height before committing.
  • 06
    Painting
    Walls and ceilings are painted — typically two to three coats. Painting is done before carpentry is installed so that walls are complete before built-ins go in. If you want smooth walls in addition to standard paint, discuss plastering or skimming with your ID early — walls are not automatically smooth, especially in resale units.
    Smooth walls require additional skimming works — this is not included by default, voice it up early
  • 07
    Carpentry
    From measurement to discussion to fabrication to delivery to installation, carpentry typically takes 3–4 weeks depending on the amount of work. Drawings and material selections must be confirmed before fabrication begins. Changes after fabrication has started are costly and may not be possible — miscommunication at this stage is the most common source of disputes.
    Review all carpentry drawings carefully before sign-off — confirm materials, dimensions, and internal layout
  • 08
    Final Fixes & Handover
    Lights, fans, accessories, silicon sealing, and touch-ups are completed. A joint walkthrough with your ID is done — any items to be attended to are listed and an agreed timeline for completion is set. The final 5% payment is released at this stage upon sign-off. Prompt sign-off after items are rectified closes the project cleanly for both you and your ID.
Typical payment milestones

Standard milestone structure used in most renovation contracts in Singapore:

10%

Renovation works typically run 8–12 weeks, excluding weekends, public holidays, and the first 15 days of Chinese New Year. Timelines may extend due to variation orders, authority approvals, or material delays — these are outside the ID's control.

04

What to Expect — & What's Normal

These are the observations homeowners most commonly raise during and after renovation. Knowing what's industry-normal versus what's a genuine defect prevents unnecessary friction — and helps you focus on what actually matters.

What looks like a problem — but is normal
What you noticeThe realityWhen to raise it
Tile colour looks inconsistent across the floor Tiles from the same batch can vary slightly in shade due to kiln temperature variations during manufacturing. This is especially noticeable in large, uniform tiles under raking light. If patches are visibly different at normal standing distance under room lighting — raise it. If it's only visible when comparing tiles side by side — it's within normal range.
Grout colour is uneven or looks patchy after drying Grout changes colour as it cures, and dries at different rates depending on ambient humidity. Grout from different bags can also vary slightly in shade. Judge after grout is fully cured (48–72 hours). Persistent patchiness after curing can be flagged.
Walls feel slightly bumpy or uneven to the touch Standard plaster has a tolerance of ±3mm over 2 metres. Walls are not automatically smooth — smooth walls require additional skimming works, which must be specifically requested and quoted. Raise this before painting starts if smoothness matters to you. After painting, skim-coat touch-ups are much harder to execute cleanly.
Paint has slight roller texture, especially in raking light Latex paint applied by roller leaves a slight texture — this is normal. It becomes more visible when light hits at an angle. The same walls look perfectly smooth under normal overhead lighting. Judge walls standing at 1.5m under normal room lighting — not with a torch at an angle. Final coat quality is what counts, not the first or second coat.
Small gap between top of carpentry and ceiling Plywood and board expand and contract with humidity. A small gap is expected and is sealed with silicon. The silicon line is the intended finish — it is not a shortcut. Silicon that is pulling away, mouldy, or missing entirely is a defect. A silicon line that is slightly wavy is normal.
Some tiles sound hollow when tapped Up to 20% hollow area is the accepted industry standard for wall tiles. Some hollow sound does not mean the tile will fail. Floor tiles should be fully bonded — hollow floor tiles can crack underfoot and should be flagged. Wall tiles with more than 20% hollow area can also be raised.
Paint colour on wall looks darker or different from the sample card Colour cards are viewed in neutral light. Your room's lighting type, ceiling height, and adjacent wall colours all affect how a paint colour reads. This is perception, not a paint defect. Always test a sample pot on your actual wall before sign-off on colour selection. Request this before painting begins.
Wardrobe doors are slightly misaligned after a few weeks Hinges and tracks adjust to humidity and the weight of doors over the first weeks. Minor misalignment early on is expected. Request one adjustment visit — this is standard within the warranty period. If doors still don't close flush after adjustment, raise it as a defect.
Marble, granite, or timber veneer looks different from the showroom sample Natural materials are quarried or cut from nature — each slab or piece has unique grain, veining, and colour variation. No two pieces are identical. Shade and pattern will differ from catalogue images. View your actual slab or sample before installation is confirmed. Once installed, natural variation is not grounds for replacement.
Measurements of built-ins differ slightly from drawings Carpentry drawings are produced before site conditions are fully known. Final measurements are taken on site, and minor dimensional differences from drawings are a normal part of the process. If a built-in is significantly shorter or doesn't reach the wall or ceiling as agreed, raise it in writing at the carpentry review stage — before finishing is applied.
What IS a genuine defect
ItemWhat qualifies as a defect
Floor tilesAny hollow tile, cracked tile body, or tile with edge height difference (lippage) exceeding 1.5mm between adjacent tiles
WaterproofingWater seeping through to ceiling below, to adjacent walls, or outside the wet area boundary
CarpentryDoors that won't close flush, gaps at seams exceeding 3mm, drawers that won't run smoothly after adjustment
ElectricalSockets that trip, flicker, or are not live at handover
Paint (final coat)Visible bare patches, peeling, or incorrect colour after final coat is completed
SiliconGaps or missing sections, pulling away from surface, mould within warranty period
Windows (if changed)Frame gaps where wall was not made good after old frame removal, visible gaps to outside
When windows are changed — check this
  • When an existing window frame is removed and a new one installed, the surrounding wall area often needs to be plastered or made good
  • This wall-making-good work is not automatically included — confirm with your ID before window works begin
  • Gaps between the new frame and the wall, or uneven plaster around the new window, should be attended to before painting
The golden rule

Judge workmanship standing at normal distance under room lighting — not with a torch pressed against the surface.

A torch will reveal imperfections on any wall, in any home, anywhere in the world. The right question is: does it look right when I'm living in it? That is the standard your ID is held to.

Warranty to expect from your ID
24 months
Workmanship warranty — covers defects in construction and installation quality
12 months
Waterproofing warranty — applies to all hacked wet areas that have been re-waterproofed

Warranty only takes effect after all payments are settled and the handover certificate is signed. Natural wear and tear, misuse, and modifications by third parties are excluded.

What Adds Cost

These are the most common reasons renovation costs go beyond the initial quote. Most are design upgrades — which is fine, as long as you know they're coming.

What triggers itWhy the cost goes upRough impact
Upgrading from acrylic/laminate doors to glass doors Tempered glass fabrication, aluminium framing, and installation are more labour-intensive than standard laminate casement doors +$300–$800/door set
Adding false ceiling with features Each feature — cove lighting, layered bulkheads, curves, stepped levels — adds material and labour. A flat false ceiling is cheapest; decorative features compound quickly. +$800–$3,000/feature
Premium countertop material Sintered stone and natural stone cost significantly more than laminate. Thicker profiles, larger slabs, and complex edge profiles add further. 2–4× laminate cost
Feature wall (laminate, stone, or wallpaper) Full-height feature walls are large in area and require clean, flush backing. Material and labour both contribute. $1,500–$5,000 depending on material
Tile selection above standard allowance Standard tile budgets (typically up to $3.50/psf) cover a wide range. Imported tiles, large-format slabs, and textured tiles cost more per psf and have higher waste factors. Varies — can double tiling cost
Large-format floor tiles (600×1200mm or larger) Requires a more level screed, more cuts per row, and longer laying time. More material is wasted per cut piece. +30–50% vs 600×600mm
Adding display cabinets, dry pantry, or study desk Every additional carpentry item adds to fabrication, delivery, and installation cost. Aluminium system cabinets (e.g. pole systems) are priced differently from standard wood-based carpentry. $500–$3,000 per unit
Concealed works where exposed was originally quoted Concealing electrical or plumbing works requires additional hacking, conduit, and plastering. If the original quote assumed exposed, concealing is a variation order. Quoted as additional VO
Double-glazed windows vs standard aluminium Double glazing significantly reduces noise and heat — but the glass cost is much higher. Common upgrade for flats facing busy roads or MRT lines. ~2× standard window cost
Design changes after carpentry fabrication begins Once fabrication starts, cut materials cannot be reused. Changes at this stage mean new material, new labour, and possible delay to delivery timeline. High — material cost may not be recoverable
Any item not stated in the original contract Anything added during renovation that was not in the signed contract is a Variation Order (VO) and will be charged separately at the time it is agreed Priced per VO

07

Furniture & Appliances

These decisions feel like they can wait — but they can't. Appliances affect carpentry drawings. Furniture layout affects where your electrical points go. Decide early.

Decide in this order
  • 1. Appliance brands and models — exact cutout dimensions must be confirmed before cabinet drawings are finalised
  • 2. Built-in vs loose furniture scope — determines how many electrical points are needed and where
  • 3. Order appliances with long lead times — imported or built-in appliances can take 6–10 weeks. A delay here stalls carpentry.
  • 4. Loose furniture layout — confirm before electrical concealing works begin, so TV points, fan points, and socket positions are correct
  • 5. Measure lift car and doorways — before ordering any large furniture or appliance for delivery
Air-conditioning & lighting — plan with your ID
Air-conditioning
  • Confirm the number of indoor units, brand, and system type before works begin — your ID will coordinate the trunking and DB point allocation
  • System 4 (4 indoor units, 1 compressor) is standard for most 4–5 room HDB flats
  • If you plan a false ceiling, the condenser pipe routing needs to be decided before ceiling boards go up
  • Include an access panel in false ceiling design for future servicing — your ID will advise on placement
Lighting
  • Plan your lighting scheme with your ID — recessed downlights, pendant, cove, track, and strip lights all have different electrical requirements
  • Warm white (2,700–3,000K) suits bedrooms and living areas. Cooler white (4,000K) works better in kitchens and study rooms.
  • Decide pendant light positions only after your dining table size is confirmed — position is tied to table centre
  • Dimmable circuits can be added where ambience matters — discuss this when confirming electrical points
